3. Pain Points

Operational

  1. Four service lines, one manual operation. Plumbing, electrical, roofing, and solar each carry their own scheduling, quoting, and compliance requirements. Manual coordination across all four creates bottlenecks at every handoff.

  2. Quoting and job management is time-intensive. Quote generation across multiple trade types, job tracking, and compliance documentation are high-volume, rules-based tasks consuming significant bandwidth daily.

  3. Compliance burden. Trade businesses carry heavy compliance obligations. Document processing, licensing tracking, and job record management eat hours of admin per week.

Marketing

  1. Marketing ROI is unclear. Investment across multiple digital channels with no clear attribution from spend to booked jobs and revenue.

  2. Heavy agency dependency. Digital marketing and reporting currently outsourced. Goal is to progressively build in-house capability across SEO, SEM, Google Ads, Meta Ads, EDM, and reporting, reducing cost and dependency.

Systems

  1. No unified layer. ServiceTitan, Xero, Podium, WageLock, Asset Panda all operate in silos. Manual reconciliation between systems.

  2. AI-ready founder, no unified automation layer yet. Paul has shown strong commitment and genuine intent from the first conversation. The opportunity is deploying the right automation infrastructure across the existing platform stack — connecting what is already in place rather than starting from scratch.

4. Growth Constraint Framework

The rule of growth: when lead and growth marketing stall, there are only three possible bottlenecks. Find which one is binding, fix it, then find the next one.

The Three Constraints

     1. AWARENESS              2. CONVERSION              3. OPERATIONS
    ┌──────────────┐         ┌──────────────┐         ┌──────────────┐
    │ Are we        │         │ Are we        │         │ How fast are  │
    │ getting       │    →    │ converting    │    →    │ we getting    │
    │ enough        │         │ the leads     │         │ through the   │
    │ leads?        │         │ we get?       │         │ back end?     │
    └──────────────┘         └──────────────┘         └──────────────┘
         ↑                        ↑                        ↑
    Channel mix,              Close rates,              Booking → job
    spend levels,             quote quality,            completion time,
    reach                     sales process             system friction

How to diagnose: The first thing is understanding the data. What data are we collecting around lead awareness, conversions, and back-end process? Once we have the data, we determine the baselines and where the bottlenecks are in the whole process. If we don't have the data, that is where we start: how do we collect it?

The Sequencing Rule

If awareness and conversion are working but growth is still slow, the constraint is in operations: how quickly are we getting from booking through to job completion? Is there time wasted in the systems we are using?

For a multi-trade business, operations constraint is highly likely. Four service lines, manual quoting/scheduling, compliance overhead, the back end is where friction compounds.

Working Backwards from Business Goals

If the goal is X thousand jobs in the next year: 1. How many leads are needed, given current conversion rate, for that to happen? 2. Where are those leads coming from currently? 3. What channels are actually working? 4. If we don't have tracking in place, that is step one, get baselines to see what's working and what's not.

Core principle: Do not optimise anything in isolation. Understand what is going to move the needle for the business goals. Critical numbers are the measure of success.

6. Customer Insights (foundational)

This is the piece most operators skip. Before optimising channels, you need to know who you are optimising for.

What to ask / investigate

Question Why
Who is our best customer? Segment by revenue, frequency, margin
What are their jobs to be done? What task are they hiring the service to complete?
What are their motivations? Why did they pick up the phone?
What are their barriers? What almost stopped them from booking?
What are their pain points? What problem were they trying to solve?

What to build

  • Customer lifetime value analysis, run over the CRM if it hasn't been done
  • Retention and returning customer rate, how much is repeat vs. new
  • Dashboards, one place tracking all of the above

This data informs everything downstream: which channels to invest in, which customers to target, what messaging resonates. Without it, marketing is guessing.

Likely state of play (confirmed by investigation)

The initial assessment identified unclear marketing ROI and heavy agency reliance. Live investigation on 28-29 June confirms:

  • Confirmed: 24 Google Ads running. Conversion tracking exists on service domains (AW-861810151 verified on abaplumbing.com.au) but each domain uses separate GA4 properties — fragmented, not absent. The agencies can track conversions per-domain but cannot produce a unified cross-service view. No GTM — tracking is hard-coded on individual pages, making it brittle and hard to audit.
  • Other service domains (electrical, roofing, solar, air conditioning) not yet inspected — tracking may exist on some, may be missing on others
  • No UTM discipline across campaigns
  • ServiceTitan lead source tracking does not yet appear to be configured
  • Customer insight analysis is an untapped opportunity — best customer profile, LTV, and retention rates would create immediate value with the right data infrastructure in place
  • Marketing data is distributed across multiple domains with separate tracking properties — there is no unified attribution view connecting spend to revenue across all service lines

The fix (what Josh proposes)

  1. Audit the full tracking stack across all service domains, 2-3 days. Map every pixel, tag, and conversion event on every domain (plumbing, electrical, roofing, solar, air conditioning, plus the main site). Catalog what exists, what's missing, and where the gaps are.
  2. Unify the data. Consolidate multiple GA4 properties and conversion tags into one attribution view. The plumbing domain's AW-861810151 is already firing — surface that data alongside the other domains.
  3. Get the baselines, GA4 + Clarity + agent synthesis. Understand what channels are working across all service lines before changing anything.
  4. Wire ServiceTitan to attribution, every booked job carries a lead source. ServiceTitan's Marketing Ads API already records job attributions — the data exists inside the platform, just needs surfacing.
  5. Run customer insight analysis, best customer, LTV, retention, JTBD. Over the CRM.
  6. Build a single dashboard, one place for spend, leads, jobs, revenue, cost per acquisition, and customer metrics — across all service lines. Updated automatically.
  7. Then, and only then, decide which channels to keep outsourced and which to bring in-house. Data first, decisions second.

Data Protection & Governance Framework

This is the operating standard, not an afterthought. Data protection and sovereignty are foundational commitments.

Access & Permissions

  • Define and maintain access permissions across all platforms — ServiceTitan, Xero, Podium, Google Ads, Meta Ads, WordPress, Asset Panda
  • Per-user, revocable access. Every API key lives behind a control layer. No credential is ever exposed to an end user. Access can be revoked in seconds — not days. Pattern production-tested at AFS Group across 18 platforms with zero credential exposure

Infrastructure

  • Dedicated cloud instance on orgo.ai. Each Snowark deployment runs in its own isolated Docker container — not shared infrastructure. Paul's agent lives in its own environment, with its own file system, its own credential store. No other client's data or processes touch it
  • DeepSeek on Hermes. The agent runs on the Hermes framework with DeepSeek as the reasoning model. Prompts are processed via API — responses are generated and returned. DeepSeek does not use customer data for model training
  • Snowark operates the infrastructure; Paul owns everything on it. Josh manages the deployment, keeps it running, and handles upgrades. Paul owns the agent, the data, the workflows, and the credentials

Data Residency & Privacy

  • Tenant-isolated. ABA Trades' agent runs in a dedicated container. No data commingling. No shared databases. The instance is single-tenant by design
  • Agent operations comply with Australian data privacy and security standards
  • Audit trails maintained for all agent actions and decisions — who did what, when, and with what data
  • Paul can revoke everything, instantly. Access to every platform runs through the agent's credential store. Cut one key and the agent loses access to that platform. Cut the orgo instance and everything stops. No data leaves Paul's control without his explicit approval

Escalation & Decision Authority

  • Any role-redundancy or fundamental workflow-change recommendation is escalated to Paul for collective decision. The agent never fires anyone
  • Living agent registry: Paul can see what's live at any time — status, business impact, dependencies
  • Quarterly reviews with Paul: what's running, what it's delivering, what's planned next, risks and failures

Budget & Infrastructure

  • Dedicated P&L line: "AI & Automation" under software expenses. Goal: net reduction in total software + wage spend, not net addition
  • Monthly API/token budget, tracked and reported. No surprise bills

13. Key Discussion Areas

Q1: "Walk me through the most complex AI system you've built and deployed in a real business."

Anchor: Lead with Gary, the production AI agent Josh built at AFS Group. Not a pilot , 8 autonomous processes running across 18 platforms (Gmail, Asana, HubSpot, Shopify, NetSuite, GA4, Meta Ads, Google Ads). 24 packaged skills. 40+ persistent memory files with nightly consolidation. 3,447 sales calls analysed across two brands, codified into 14 data points per call and fed into a live executive dashboard that now drives budget allocation. Custom MCP server architecture , Cloudflare Workers exposing Shopify API with read-only scoping, per-user access, zero credential exposure. Standalone tools built and deployed: SOP generator (video→process doc), competitor price scraper (daily Python automation), Chrome extensions for voice dictation and screen recording. Built while running three brands as CMO with $6.2M budget. SaaS replacement: agent-maintained HubSpot-to-Klaviyo sync replaced a paid Zapier subscription.

For trades context: Not Forgotten Systems (Atlas) , automated quote follow-up deploying the same agent architecture on orgo.ai. $150k+ recovered revenue. The Snowark deploy kit extends this pattern to multi-service operations with scheduling, quoting, and compliance out of the box.

Q2: "If you were starting here on Monday, what's the first thing you'd want to understand?"

Anchor: The data. What data are we collecting around lead awareness, conversions, and back-end process? The rule of growth says there are only three possible constraints: awareness, conversion, or operations. I need to see which one is binding. If we don't have the data, that's where I start, how do we collect it, what does the baseline look like? I also need to understand the business strategy: what are the key priorities that move the needle? I don't optimise anything in isolation. Critical numbers are the measure of success.

Then I walk the workflow, how a lead becomes a job and how a job becomes cash, with the person who does it, for each service line. I want to see where the friction lives, where things get re-entered, where the paper stops.

Q3: "How would you approach mapping workflows across four service lines in two weeks?"

Anchor: One service line per 2-3 days. Shadow the person who does the work. Document the steps. Identify where the same logic repeats across lines, quoting structure, compliance checks, scheduling rules are likely the same pattern with different line items. The goal is not four separate automations; it's one automation layer with four configurations.

Q4: "How would you assess whether to bring marketing in-house vs. keeping it outsourced?"

Anchor: I have done both and I still use a mix. Media buying, Google Ads in particular, I would probably outsource to an agency. Being an expert day in, day out across multiple brands is an advantage for ad placement, they do not need deep business knowledge to be good at it. Social media is different: it needs to be close to the business, based on core principles, deeply entwined. That should be in-house. Videography, probably part-time in-house.

But all of this depends on the baseline. I need to understand what channels are currently working before I recommend changing anything. If we don't have tracking in Analytics, that's step one. We need to get baselines to see what's actually working and what's not. Once we can see cost per acquired job by channel, the build vs. buy decision makes itself.

I wouldn't recommend expensive attribution software like Triple Whale at this stage. GA4 is fine, Microsoft Clarity is good, and getting an agent to synthesise data from those systems is a very effective way to start.

The principle: near-term value comes from bolting agents onto existing processes. The durable value comes later, once we've proven the model and earned the right to reframe how the business operates.
