4. Growth Constraint Framework

The rule of growth: when lead and growth marketing stall, there are only three possible bottlenecks. Find which one is binding, fix it, then find the next one.

The Three Constraints

     1. AWARENESS              2. CONVERSION              3. OPERATIONS
    ┌──────────────┐         ┌──────────────┐         ┌──────────────┐
    │ Are we        │         │ Are we        │         │ How fast are  │
    │ getting       │    →    │ converting    │    →    │ we getting    │
    │ enough        │         │ the leads     │         │ through the   │
    │ leads?        │         │ we get?       │         │ back end?     │
    └──────────────┘         └──────────────┘         └──────────────┘
         ↑                        ↑                        ↑
    Channel mix,              Close rates,              Booking → job
    spend levels,             quote quality,            completion time,
    reach                     sales process             system friction

How to diagnose: The first thing is understanding the data. What data are we collecting around lead awareness, conversions, and back-end process? Once we have the data, we determine the baselines and where the bottlenecks are in the whole process. If we don't have the data, that is where we start: how do we collect it?

The Sequencing Rule

If awareness and conversion are working but growth is still slow, the constraint is in operations: how quickly are we getting from booking through to job completion? Is there time wasted in the systems we are using?

For a multi-trade business, operations constraint is highly likely. Four service lines, manual quoting/scheduling, compliance overhead, the back end is where friction compounds.

Working Backwards from Business Goals

If the goal is X thousand jobs in the next year: 1. How many leads are needed, given current conversion rate, for that to happen? 2. Where are those leads coming from currently? 3. What channels are actually working? 4. If we don't have tracking in place, that is step one, get baselines to see what's working and what's not.

Core principle: Do not optimise anything in isolation. Understand what is going to move the needle for the business goals. Critical numbers are the measure of success.

6. Customer Insights (foundational)

This is the piece most operators skip. Before optimising channels, you need to know who you are optimising for.

What to ask / investigate

Question Why
Who is our best customer? Segment by revenue, frequency, margin
What are their jobs to be done? What task are they hiring the service to complete?
What are their motivations? Why did they pick up the phone?
What are their barriers? What almost stopped them from booking?
What are their pain points? What problem were they trying to solve?

What to build

  • Customer lifetime value analysis, run over the CRM if it hasn't been done
  • Retention and returning customer rate, how much is repeat vs. new
  • Dashboards, one place tracking all of the above

This data informs everything downstream: which channels to invest in, which customers to target, what messaging resonates. Without it, marketing is guessing.

  • [ ] Call tracking, phone calls attributed to the channel that generated them
  • [ ] CRM lead source tracking, ServiceTitan recording lead source so jobs can be traced to channel
  • [ ] Dashboard, single view: spend → leads → booked jobs → revenue, per channel. Plus customer LTV, retention, and repeat rate.

The fix (what Josh proposes)

  2. Get the baselines, GA4 + Clarity + agent synthesis. Understand what channels are working before changing anything.

  6. Then, and only then, decide which channels to keep outsourced and which to bring in-house. Data first, decisions second.

Decision Rule

13. Interview Questions, Josh's Anchors

Q1: "Walk me through the most complex AI system you've built and deployed in a real business."

Anchor: Lead with Gary, the production AI agent Josh built at AFS Group. Not a pilot , 8 autonomous processes running across 18 platforms (Gmail, Asana, HubSpot, Shopify, NetSuite, GA4, Meta Ads, Google Ads). 24 packaged skills. 40+ persistent memory files with nightly consolidation. 3,447 sales calls analysed across two brands, codified into 14 data points per call and fed into a live executive dashboard that now drives budget allocation. Custom MCP server architecture , Cloudflare Workers exposing Shopify API with read-only scoping, per-user access, zero credential exposure. Standalone tools built and deployed: SOP generator (video→process doc), competitor price scraper (daily Python automation), Chrome extensions for voice dictation and screen recording. Built while running three brands as CMO with $6.2M budget. SaaS replacement: agent-maintained HubSpot-to-Klaviyo sync replaced a paid Zapier subscription.

For trades context: Not Forgotten Systems (Atlas) , automated quote follow-up deploying the same agent architecture on orgo.ai. $150k+ recovered revenue. The Snowark deploy kit extends this pattern to multi-service operations with scheduling, quoting, and compliance out of the box.

Q2: "If you were starting here on Monday, what's the first thing you'd want to understand?"

Anchor: The data. What data are we collecting around lead awareness, conversions, and back-end process? The rule of growth says there are only three possible constraints: awareness, conversion, or operations. I need to see which one is binding. If we don't have the data, that's where I start, how do we collect it, what does the baseline look like? I also need to understand the business strategy: what are the key priorities that move the needle? I don't optimise anything in isolation. Critical numbers are the measure of success.

Then I walk the workflow, how a lead becomes a job and how a job becomes cash, with the person who does it, for each service line. I want to see where the friction lives, where things get re-entered, where the paper stops.

Q3: "How would you approach mapping workflows across four service lines in two weeks?"

Anchor: One service line per 2-3 days. Shadow the person who does the work. Document the steps. Identify where the same logic repeats across lines, quoting structure, compliance checks, scheduling rules are likely the same pattern with different line items. The goal is not four separate automations; it's one automation layer with four configurations.

Q4: "How would you assess whether to bring marketing in-house vs. keeping it outsourced?"

Anchor: I have done both and I still use a mix. Media buying, Google Ads in particular, I would probably outsource to an agency. Being an expert day in, day out across multiple brands is an advantage for ad placement, they do not need deep business knowledge to be good at it. Social media is different: it needs to be close to the business, based on core principles, deeply entwined. That should be in-house. Videography, probably part-time in-house.

But all of this depends on the baseline. I need to understand what channels are currently working before I recommend changing anything. If we don't have tracking in Analytics, that's step one. We need to get baselines to see what's actually working and what's not. Once we can see cost per acquired job by channel, the build vs. buy decision makes itself.

I wouldn't recommend expensive attribution software like Triple Whale at this stage. GA4 is fine, Microsoft Clarity is good, and getting an agent to synthesise data from those systems is a very effective way to start.

Q5: "What does your first 90 days look like?"

Anchor: Four phases with stage gates , this is a designed rollout, not a project plan.

Phase 1 (Weeks 1-4): Foundations. Extend agent infrastructure, confirm platform access, run structured audits with every department head. Produce a ranked automation pipeline , top 5-10 opportunities by ROI, hours saved, and feasibility. Audit the marketing tracking stack. Run customer insight baseline over the CRM. Phase 2 (Weeks 5-12): First bolt-on wins. Deploy the first 1-2 automated workflows from the pipeline. Ship a revenue-positive agentic A/B test against a manual baseline , likely quoting. The principle: near-term value comes from bolting agents onto existing processes. The durable value comes later, once we've proven the model and earned the right to reframe how the business operates.
